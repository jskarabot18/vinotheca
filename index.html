A Library of Wine Tools

Vinotheca

Atlases, maps, and a personal codex — for the curious drinker.

❧

assembled by Jure Skarabot · New York

Wine rewards close attention. A bottle is a piece of geography, a piece of history, and a small argument about taste — and the more ways you can look at it, the more it gives back.

The wine world is full of scores and full of prose, but surprisingly short of tools that help a curious drinker see patterns, find connections, and keep honest track of what they have tasted. The instruments collected here try to fill that gap. Each one answers a specific question a wine-curious person actually asks: where are the great sites for the grape I love; which lesser-known varieties are likely to win me over next; why do some regions on opposite sides of the world feel like cousins; and which of my own tasting notes can I actually trust?

An atlas of great vineyards. A map of grape kinship. A study of what gives a region its character. And a personal codex of bottles tasted. Maps where maps help; charts where charts help; prose where neither will do.

ii.

TasteRank Explorer

A map of how grape varieties relate to one another.

If you love Nebbiolo, which lesser-known grape is likely to win you over next? If Syrah from Côte-Rôtie is a fixed point on your map, what else should you be exploring? TasteRank plots 101 varieties as a network — each grape a node, each connection a shared sensory signature — and lets you wander the constellations. Six natural neighbourhoods emerge from the data: the floral aromatics, the dark structural reds, the mineral whites, and three others waiting to be found.

iii.

The Soul of Wine

Why two regions on opposite continents can feel like the same place.

Alsace and Santa Barbara grow some of the same grapes on cool slopes near cool water, yet they could not feel more different: one rooted in centuries of inheritance and tradition, the other young, inventive, still writing its own story. Fifty-nine regions in sixteen countries, each one portrayed twice: once by its land, once by its spirit. The two portraits don't agree, and that disagreement is where the good wine stories live. Six distinct ways a wine region can have a soul.

Usage

What Each Tool Does

in plain terms

The Grand Cru Atlas

A reference atlas of the world's finest vineyards, organised by grape variety rather than by country. Good for answering a simple question: if I want the best expressions of Nebbiolo, or Riesling, or Syrah, where on earth are they grown? Browsable online, or downloadable as a single bound volume.

TasteRank Explorer

A visual map that groups grape varieties by how they actually taste, not by colour or continent. If you love one grape, it shows you which others are closest to it in sensory character — a recommendation engine built on flavour rather than price or popularity. Six natural families emerge from the data, each worth exploring.

The Soul of Wine

A study of what gives a wine region its identity. Every region is portrayed twice — once by its land (soil, climate, geography) and once by its culture (tradition, ambition, history) — and the two portraits are compared. The surprising finding is that they often disagree, and the places where they disagree are frequently the most interesting regions to explore.

Codex Vini

A personal tasting record: every wine tasted, every rating, every vintage, filterable by country, grape, colour, or score. An adjustment built into the averages keeps a single outstanding bottle from overpraising an entire country — so the rankings become more honest as more wines are added. The working end of the library, and the one that grows.
