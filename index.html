A Library of Wine Tools

Vinotheca

Atlases, maps, and a personal codex — for the curious drinker.

❧

assembled by Jure Skarabot · New York

Wine rewards close attention. A bottle is a piece of geography, a piece of history, and a small argument about taste — and the more ways you can look at it, the more it gives back.

The instruments collected here are an attempt to look at wine from several directions at once. An atlas of great vineyards. A map of grape kinship. A study of what gives a region its character. And a personal codex of bottles tasted. Maps where maps help; charts where charts help; prose where neither will do.

TasteRank Explorer

A map of how grape varieties relate to one another.

If you love Nebbiolo, which lesser-known grape is likely to win you over next? TasteRank plots 101 varieties as a network — each grape a node, each connection a shared sensory signature — and lets you wander the constellations. Six natural neighbourhoods emerge from the data: the floral aromatics, the dark structural reds, the mineral whites, and three others waiting to be found.

The Soul of Wine

Why two regions on opposite continents can feel like the same place.

Burgundy and the Santa Cruz Mountains share no geology, no climate, no grapes — and yet they feel like cousins. Why? Fifty-nine regions in sixteen countries, each one portrayed twice: once by its land, once by its spirit. The two portraits don't agree, and that disagreement is where the good wine stories live. Six distinct ways a wine region can have a soul.

Apparatus

On Approach

four convictions that shape the library

The map matters, up to a point

Geography explains a great deal about wine — which grapes ripen where, which soils give which textures, which slopes catch which sun. An atlas remains the right starting place. What follows the atlas is what's interesting.

Flavour is a network, not a list

The old taxonomies arrange grapes by colour or continent. A more honest picture groups them by what they actually taste like — and the groupings that emerge cut clean across the map. Some of the best recommendations follow these lines.

A region has two identities

One belongs to the land: climate, stone, latitude. The other belongs to the people: ambition, tradition, the stories a region tells about itself. These rarely line up. Most interesting wine regions are interesting precisely where they come apart.

A tasting record is a living document

Ratings should age. A single brilliant bottle from a country you've visited twice doesn't mean that country makes the best wine in the world. A good codex corrects for this quietly, and updates its mind as more bottles are opened.
